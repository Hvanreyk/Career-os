## Ask different questions at different levels

The single biggest preparation mistake is bringing the same questions to
every conversation. What is genuinely useful to ask an analyst is often a
poor use of time with a managing director, and vice versa.

```table
caption: Calibrating questions to seniority
headers: ["Their level", "Good questions focus on", "Avoid asking about"]
rows:
  - ["Analyst / Associate", "Recent recruiting experience, day-to-day work, what surprised them", "Firm-wide strategy or long-term market views"]
  - ["VP / Director", "How the team works, what distinguishes strong juniors, sector trends", "Basic 'what does IB do' questions"]
  - ["MD / Senior", "Career judgement, what convinces them to sponsor a junior hire", "Process logistics — that's a recruiter's job"]
```

The workspace builds a starting question list calibrated to each contact's
seniority automatically when you schedule a coffee chat — treat it as a
starting point, not a script.

## During the conversation

Listen more than you talk. A 20-30 minute chat should feel like roughly a
70/30 split in their favour. Take light notes — a phone or notebook is fine,
but ask first ("do you mind if I jot a couple of things down?") and keep it
brief enough that you are still present in the conversation.

```callout
variant: tip
title: A simple structure that works
md: >-
  Open with a genuine, prepared question (2-3 minutes). Let the conversation
  follow naturally from their answers rather than working through a rigid
  list. With five minutes left, ask one forward-looking question — "what
  would you suggest I focus on before I apply?" is almost always well
  received. Close by thanking them and asking if there is anyone else they'd
  suggest you speak with — but only if the conversation has genuinely earned
  that.
```

## Closing professionally

Do not end a good conversation with a vague "I'll keep in touch." Be
specific about what happens next, even if it is small: "I'll let you know
how the application goes" or "I'll send that through by Friday" — and then
actually do it.

```profile_example
strength: weak
title: Vague close
bullets:
  - "This was great, thanks so much, I'll keep in touch!"
verdict: No commitment, no clear next step — easy for both people to forget by next week.
```

```profile_example
strength: strong
title: Specific close
bullets:
  - "Thank you — this was really helpful, especially what you said about the graduate process. I'll send a quick thank-you and let you know how the application goes."
verdict: Names something specific they said, and commits to one concrete next step.
```

## Right after the conversation ends

Write your debrief within the hour, while it is still fresh — what you
learned, anything they offered to do (a referral, an introduction, sending
you something), and any names they mentioned. Names dropped in conversation
are often the best next contacts you will find all month; capture them
before they are lost.
