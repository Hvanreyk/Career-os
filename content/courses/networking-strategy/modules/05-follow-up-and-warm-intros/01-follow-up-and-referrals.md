## The thank-you is not optional

A thank-you within 24 hours of a conversation is the single highest-value
message in the entire networking loop — and the easiest to skip because it
feels less urgent than the "real" outreach. It should be short, mention
something specific from the conversation, and make no new ask.

```profile_example
strength: weak
title: Generic thank-you
bullets:
  - "Thanks for your time today, really appreciated it!"
verdict: Could be sent after any conversation with anyone — no evidence you were actually listening.
```

```profile_example
strength: strong
title: Specific thank-you
bullets:
  - "Thank you for the time today — your point about how the graduate process weighs the summer internship was genuinely useful, and I'll factor that into my timeline."
verdict: References something specific they said, showing the conversation mattered and was heard.
```

## Following up when there is no reply

Silence after a first message is normal, not a rejection. One brief, polite
follow-up after about a week is appropriate. More than that starts to read
as pressure rather than persistence.

```table
caption: Follow-up timing and stopping rules
headers: ["Situation", "What to do", "When to stop"]
rows:
  - ["No reply after first message", "One brief, polite follow-up around day 7", "After one follow-up with no response, let it rest"]
  - ["They replied but conversation stalled", "A short, useful update — not a repeat ask", "If two updates get no response, move on gracefully"]
  - ["After a coffee chat", "Thank-you within 24 hours, then occasional genuine updates", "No fixed limit — this becomes an ongoing relationship"]
```

The workspace's weekly plan surfaces exactly this: contacts who have gone
quiet for 7+ days get a gentle nudge to send one follow-up — never more than
one before you're prompted to let it rest.

## Warm introductions and referrals

A warm introduction — being introduced by someone who already knows the
target — converts far better than cold outreach, because trust is
transferred rather than built from zero. But it only works if you make it
easy and low-pressure for the person doing the introducing.

```callout
variant: tip
title: How to ask for an introduction
md: >-
  Ask the connecting person directly, and make it effortless: "Would you be
  comfortable introducing me to [name]? Happy to send a short paragraph you
  can forward if that's easier." Draft that forwardable paragraph yourself —
  the Message Lab's warm-intro request purpose is built for exactly this —
  so they only have to hit forward, not write anything from scratch.
```

A referral is a bigger ask than an introduction — it puts the referrer's own
name behind your application. Only request one once a real relationship
exists, and always give the person an easy way to say no. "No pressure at
all if it's not something you're comfortable with" is not just polite, it
protects the relationship regardless of their answer.

```callout
variant: note
title: What "earned" looks like
md: >-
  You do not need dozens of conversations to earn a referral — one genuine,
  well-run coffee chat where the person offered to help is enough. What
  matters is that the offer came from them, not that you extracted a
  promise. If they raise it, follow up. If they don't, do not ask directly
  unless the relationship has clearly reached that point.
```

```callout
variant: tip
title: Do this now
md: >-
  Check your workspace's **Today** view for anyone due a thank-you or a
  follow-up. If a contact offered to introduce you to someone during a
  coffee chat, record it under that contact as a warm introduction so
  nothing gets lost — and draft the forwardable paragraph while it's fresh.
```
