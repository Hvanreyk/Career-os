## The reader is busy — respect that structurally

A junior banker reads your message in seconds, often on their phone, between
meetings. The structure of a good cold email reflects that reality: it gets
to the point, states a specific and easy ask, and does not require the
reader to do any work to understand what you want.

```table
caption: A reliable cold-email structure
headers: ["Part", "Purpose", "Length"]
rows:
  - ["Subject line", "Tells them exactly what this is before they open it", "5-8 words"]
  - ["Opening line", "One truthful, specific reason you are contacting them", "1 sentence"]
  - ["Context", "Who you are and what you are doing (year, university, goal)", "1-2 sentences"]
  - ["The ask", "A specific, low-effort request — usually a short call", "1 sentence"]
  - ["Sign-off", "Name, and how they can reach you", "1 line"]
```

Aim for under 150 words. If you cannot fit a clear message into that, you are
either including unnecessary context or have not decided what you are
actually asking for.

## The ask has to be genuinely easy to say yes to

"Can I pick your brain sometime?" is vague and puts the effort of scheduling
back on the reader. "Would you have 15 minutes for a call in the next couple
of weeks — I'm flexible on timing" is specific and easy to answer. Propose a
short duration, and make it clear you will work around their schedule.

```profile_example
strength: weak
title: Vague, effortful ask
bullets:
  - "I'd love to learn more about your career whenever you have time."
verdict: No timeframe, no duration, no clear next step — the reader has to do all the work to turn this into a real meeting.
```

```profile_example
strength: strong
title: Specific, low-effort ask
bullets:
  - "Would you have 15 minutes for a call in the next two weeks? Happy to work around your schedule."
verdict: Bounded time commitment, a concrete window, and an explicit offer to accommodate them.
```

## Email and LinkedIn are different channels

```table
caption: Choosing between email and LinkedIn
headers: ["Channel", "When it works best", "Constraints"]
rows:
  - ["Email", "You have a verified address and want a considered reply", "No hard length limit, but shorter is still better"]
  - ["LinkedIn connection note", "You only have a LinkedIn profile, or want a lighter first touch", "300 characters — no room for an ask beyond connecting"]
  - ["LinkedIn message", "After connecting, or when email is unavailable", "Often read on mobile — keep it as short as email"]
```

A LinkedIn connection note has no room for anything beyond a specific,
truthful reason to connect. Save the ask for the message you send after they
accept.

## Before you send: preflight and review

Every draft in the Message Lab runs through **deterministic checks** before
any AI review — missing recipient details, leftover placeholder text like
`[Name]`, an empty ask, excessive length, or suspicious shortened links. Fix
those first. Then the **AI review** gives you qualitative feedback: real
strengths, specific issues classified by type (relevance, specificity, tone,
brevity, credibility, the ask, timing, or unintended pressure), and up to
three faithful rewrites that keep every fact you supplied and invent nothing.

```callout
variant: note
title: What the AI will never do
md: >-
  It will not score your message, predict a reply probability, or promise a
  recruiter's approval. It will not add a shared connection, a deal, or a

```
