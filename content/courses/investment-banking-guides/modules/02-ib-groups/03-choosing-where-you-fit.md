Knowing the groups is table stakes. The useful skill is mapping *yourself* onto them — honestly, and with the Australian market's structure in mind.

## Most Australian intakes are generalist

At most firms in Australia you apply to "Investment Banking" as a single program, not to a specific group. Some banks allocate you to a team after offers or after training; others run rotations. A few (typically larger offices) let you express a preference. Practical consequences:

- Your application and interviews should show you understand the *division*, not commit you irrevocably to one desk.
- "Which group interests you?" is still asked — as a test of research quality, not a binding choice.
- Team allocation is influenced by performance, need and expressed interest — another reason internships matter, since interns see teams from the inside before preferencing.

## A self-mapping framework

Rather than choosing by prestige folklore, weigh four questions:

```table
caption: Mapping your preferences to groups
headers:
  - If you...
  - Lean towards
  - Because
rows:
  - - Want maximum modelling and end-to-end deals
    - M&A, LevFin
    - Deepest valuation and transaction-structure work
  - - Are energised by live markets and fast feedback
    - ECM, DCM
    - Work is priced off today's market, deals close in days/weeks
  - - Like a specific industry's companies and drivers
    - Coverage in that sector
    - Sector depth compounds and directs your deal flow
  - - Are drawn to complex, high-stakes problem-solving
    - Restructuring, Sponsors
    - Distress and buyouts concentrate the hardest questions
```

None of these is fixed at graduate level: skills transfer, people move between groups, and two years in any of them builds the same core toolkit.
