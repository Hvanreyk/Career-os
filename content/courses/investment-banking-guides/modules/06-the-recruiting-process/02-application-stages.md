A typical structured application runs through five or six stages. Knowing what each stage is actually *filtering for* tells you how to prepare for it.

## Stage by stage

**1. Online application.** Resume, sometimes a cover letter, academic transcript, eligibility questions (working rights, graduation date). *Filters for:* academics, evidenced achievement, attention to detail, and — bluntly — whether your resume survives a 30-second scan. Many rejections happen here silently. The Resume & Cover Letter course is the preparation.

**2. Psychometric testing.** Online numerical, verbal, logical and increasingly game-based assessments; some firms add situational-judgement tests. Usually strictly timed. *Filters for:* processing speed and quantitative comfort under time pressure. Preparation genuinely moves scores: practise the common formats until the mechanics are automatic. Complete them promptly — some firms don't progress applications until testing is done.

**3. Video interview (recorded).** Pre-recorded questions, 1–3 minutes to answer each on camera, often one retry or none. Typically motivational and behavioural ("why banking, why us, tell me about a challenge"). *Filters for:* communication clarity, structure, professionalism on camera. Practise recording yourself — everyone hates it, prepared people hate it less.

**4. Phone or live-video interviews.** With HR first, then bankers. Behavioural stories, motivation, market awareness ("what's a deal you've followed?"), and first technicals — statements, valuation basics from Module 5. *Filters for:* genuine interest, structured thinking, technical floor.

**5. Assessment centre / superday.** The final round, compressing several exercises into a half-day: multiple banker interviews (behavioural + technical), often a group exercise or case study (analyse a scenario, recommend, present), sometimes a short written or modelling task. *Filters for:* how you actually operate — with people, under time pressure, with incomplete information. In group exercises the trap is dominating; the pass is contributing visibly *and* building on others.

**6. Offer.** Usually a phone call, then a formal letter with a response deadline. It's acceptable to ask for reasonable time to decide and to be honest (briefly, professionally) about other live processes.

## Interviews from the interviewer's side

Three questions sit behind nearly everything a banker asks:

1. **Can you do the work?** Technicals, attention to detail, quantitative comfort.
2. **Do you actually want this job?** Evidenced motivation — deals followed, people spoken to, an honest "why" (Module 1's self-check pays off here).
3. **Can I put you in front of my team and, eventually, a client at midnight in week eight?** Composure, likeability, professionalism.

Every preparation activity in this platform maps to one of those three.
