The sell-side process is the archetypal investment banking transaction — learn it properly and every other deal type becomes a variation. This is also the single most useful process to describe fluently in interviews.

## The setup

A client (the whole company's owners, or a parent selling a division) mandates a bank to sell the business for the best achievable combination of **price, certainty and speed**. The bank's fee is mostly a success fee on completion. The core strategic decision up front: a **broad auction** (many buyers, maximum tension, more disruption and leak risk) versus a **targeted process** (a handful of logical buyers, more discretion) versus a **bilateral negotiation** (one buyer, usually fastest and quietest).

## The stages

**1. Preparation (weeks 1–6).** The team learns the business, builds the financial model, drafts the marketing materials — a short anonymous **teaser** and a detailed **Information Memorandum (IM)** — prepares the buyer list, and often coordinates **vendor due diligence** reports. Analyst life: model builds, IM drafting, profile pages on every prospective buyer.

**2. First round (weeks 6–12).** Teasers go out; interested buyers sign **NDAs** (non-disclosure agreements) and receive the IM plus a **process letter** setting the rules and bid deadline. Buyers submit **non-binding indicative bids** — a price range plus assumptions. The team analyses bids into a comparison matrix and recommends who proceeds. Analyst life: NDA tracking, Q&A logs, bid analysis.

**3. Second round (weeks 12–20).** Shortlisted bidders get deep access: a **data room** of company documents, **management presentations**, site visits and expert sessions. Buyers do full due diligence, arrange financing, and mark up the **sale and purchase agreement (SPA)**. They then submit **binding offers** with committed funding and a marked-up contract. Analyst life: data-room management, Q&A coordination, management-presentation drafting, endless trackers.

**4. Negotiation and signing.** The client (advised by the bank and lawyers) negotiates final price and terms — often keeping two bidders in play until the last day to preserve tension. Contracts are signed and the deal is announced.

**5. Signing to completion.** Regulatory approvals (in Australia: **ACCC** for competition, **FIRB** for foreign buyers), any shareholder votes, financing funding, and completion mechanics (working-capital adjustments). Weeks to many months. Then the fee gets paid.

## Why sellers run auctions at all

Competitive tension is the seller's main price lever: a bidder who knows others are circling pays more and negotiates less. The bank manufactures and maintains that tension — through the process design, the information flow and the timetable. Understanding this single idea explains most of what the deal team does day to day.
