Equity capital markets deals move a company's ownership into (or around) the public market. The two you must understand: the IPO and the follow-on raising.

## The IPO: private company becomes listed

The client's objective: raise capital and/or let existing owners sell down, at the best sustainable price, with a successful aftermarket (nobody wants a listing that immediately trades down).

**The stages:**

1. **Preparation (months).** Appoint banks (**joint lead managers** in AU parlance), lawyers, accountants. Restructure where needed, build the financial model, resolve governance. Analysts build models and drafting-session materials.
2. **Due diligence & prospectus.** In Australia the offer document is a **prospectus** lodged with ASIC, prepared under a formal due-diligence committee process. Every material statement is verified. Analysts manage verification trackers and data.
3. **Marketing.** Research analysts (separated by information barriers) publish; the company runs a **roadshow** — management presents to institutional investors across days of back-to-back meetings. Analysts build the roadshow deck and investor targeting lists.
4. **Bookbuild & pricing.** Investors lodge orders into the book; the syndicate sets the final price where demand supports a healthy aftermarket, and allocates shares — favouring investors expected to hold, not flip.
5. **Listing.** Shares begin trading on the ASX. First-day performance is watched obsessively; a modest premium is the goal.

**Why IPOs are "window" deals:** they need weeks of stable, receptive markets. Volatility shuts the window — which is why IPO pipelines stack up in bad years and burst out in good ones.

## Follow-on raisings: listed company raises more equity

Australia is distinctive here. The workhorse structure is the **placement** — new shares sold to institutions overnight at a small discount, no prospectus required within the ASX's 15% annual placement capacity — often paired with a **share purchase plan (SPP)** for retail holders. Larger raisings use **entitlement offers** (rights issues), where all shareholders can buy new shares pro-rata; these can be underwritten and structured as accelerated offers (institutions overnight, retail over weeks).

Key concepts:

- **Discount.** New equity prices below the last close; the size of the discount signals urgency and demand.
- **Dilution.** Shareholders who don't participate own a smaller slice; entitlement structures exist to treat them fairly.
- **Underwriting.** For a fee, banks guarantee the proceeds — if investors don't buy, the underwriter does. This is one of the few moments the bank's own balance sheet is genuinely at risk.
- **Use of proceeds.** Markets react very differently to "funding a value-accretive acquisition" versus "repairing the balance sheet".
