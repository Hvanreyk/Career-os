Three more transaction types complete your map: routine debt issuance, the leveraged buyout, and what happens when it all goes wrong.

## Debt issuance and refinancing

A company issues bonds (or arranges loans) to fund operations, acquisitions or — most commonly — to **refinance** maturing debt. The DCM process is fast: update the issuer's disclosure, engage ratings agencies where relevant, announce a deal, take orders from credit investors, price off a benchmark (typically a government bond yield or swap rate **plus a credit spread**), and settle within days.

What moves the price: the issuer's credit quality (rating), the maturity, market conditions, and investor demand. When credit spreads widen across the market, every borrower's cost rises — one reason bankers watch credit markets even on M&A teams: financing conditions decide whether deals are affordable.

**Refinancing risk** is the quiet danger: a company that must roll over debt in a hostile market can be forced into expensive terms — or towards restructuring.

## The leveraged buyout (LBO)

A private equity firm buys a company using a modest slice of its own fund's **equity** and a large slice of **debt** raised against the target's own cash flows. Post-deal, the company pays down that debt from its earnings; at exit (a sale or IPO, typically 3–7 years later) the equity holders capture the value created.

Returns come from three levers:

1. **Debt paydown** — every dollar of debt repaid becomes a dollar of equity value.
2. **Earnings growth** — running the business better, add-on acquisitions.
3. **Multiple expansion** — selling at a higher valuation multiple than entry (partly market luck).

What makes a good LBO target: stable, predictable cash flows (to service debt), limited capex needs, a defensible market position, and a credible improvement plan. Why interest rates matter so much: higher rates mean more cash consumed by interest, less debt capacity, lower prices PE can pay — and slower deal activity. Banks appear on every side: advising targets or sponsors (M&A/Sponsors), and arranging the debt (LevFin).

## Restructuring

When a company can't sustain its debts — a covenant breach looms, a refinancing fails, cash runs short — restructuring advisers step in for the company or for its creditors. The toolkit: **amend and extend** (renegotiate terms and maturities), asset sales, new money (emergency equity or loans), **debt-for-equity swaps** (creditors become owners), formal schemes of arrangement, or insolvency processes (in Australia: voluntary administration and receivership).

The analytical core is a brutal question answered with a model: *how much cash is there, how long will it last, and what is each stakeholder's recovery under each option?* Different creditors rank differently (secured before unsecured before equity), so restructuring is as much negotiation as analysis — every stakeholder fights for their layer of the **waterfall**.
