The final lesson of this module is a filter — not for whether you are "good enough", but for whether the actual job matches what you want. Answering this honestly now makes every later module more useful, and it is the raw material for the "why investment banking?" answer every interviewer asks.

## What the job rewards

Investment banking analysts do detailed, deadline-driven work in small teams for demanding clients. The role rewards:

- **Stamina and reliability.** Hours are genuinely long, and the work arrives unevenly — a live deal can consume evenings and weekends. (Module 3 covers this honestly; the lifestyle cost is real and you should weigh it, not romanticise it.)
- **Precision.** A pitch book with a wrong number damages the team's credibility. People who find satisfaction in getting details exactly right do better than people who find it tedious.
- **Commercial curiosity.** The analysts who progress fastest actually care why one company buys another and what it's worth — market awareness is a skill you build daily, not cram before interviews.
- **Team tolerance.** You work in tight teams under senior review, taking heavy feedback ("comments") on everything you produce. Ego is expensive.

What it does *not* especially reward early on: creativity for its own sake, autonomy, or public credit. Analysts execute; judgement comes with seniority.

## The honest trade

The classic case *for* doing it: unmatched training density (modelling, valuation, how deals really work), strong pay for a graduate role, and doors it opens — private equity, corporate development, funds, startups — because employers trust the skill base. The case *against*: the hours crowd out much of life for a few years, some of the work is repetitive, and none of it is worth it if you have no interest in companies and markets. Both cases are true simultaneously. The question is which trade you want to make.

## A quick self-check

Nobody scores perfectly on the job's demands, and this isn't a test you pass or fail. But if most of these are false for you, look hard at the alternatives from the last lesson before investing a year in IB recruiting:

- I can name a recent Australian deal and say why it interested me.
- Detail-heavy work satisfies me more than it drains me.
- I'd rather work on a transaction with a definite outcome than an open-ended strategy question.
- I am willing to trade most weeknights (and some weekends) for the first two years.
- I want the specific doors IB opens — not just "a good job in finance".
