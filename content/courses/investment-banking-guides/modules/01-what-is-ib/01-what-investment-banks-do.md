Before you can decide whether investment banking is for you — or explain in an interview why it is — you need a precise answer to a deceptively simple question: what does an investment bank actually sell?

## Advisers and intermediaries, not lenders

An investment bank is a professional services firm for major financial transactions. Its clients are companies, governments and investors — not everyday consumers. When a mining company wants to sell a business unit, when a retailer wants to list on the ASX, or when a super fund wants to buy an infrastructure asset, an investment bank is hired to advise on and execute that transaction.

That is the core distinction from a commercial bank. A commercial bank takes deposits and lends money — it puts its own balance sheet to work. An investment bank primarily sells **advice and execution**: it earns fees for helping clients complete transactions, rather than interest from lending. (Large universal banks do both, which is one reason the terms get confused.)

## The two things clients pay for

Almost everything an investment bank does for clients falls into two buckets:

**1. Advisory.** The bank advises a client on a transaction — most commonly a merger or acquisition (M&A). On a sale, the bank runs the process: valuing the business, preparing marketing materials, approaching buyers, running due diligence and negotiating terms. On an acquisition, it advises on what to pay and how to structure and finance the deal. Advisory fees are typically a percentage of the transaction value, usually paid on completion — which is why bankers care so much about deals actually closing.

**2. Financing (capital raising).** The bank helps a client raise money from investors. That can be **equity** — an initial public offering (IPO) or a follow-on raising by an already-listed company — or **debt**, such as issuing bonds or arranging loans. Here the bank acts as an intermediary between the client and investors: structuring the offer, setting the price, and using its distribution network to place the securities. Fees are typically a percentage of the amount raised.

## Why companies hire investment banks

Clients are sophisticated — many have their own M&A or treasury teams. They still pay investment banks millions per transaction for a few concrete reasons:

- **Process expertise.** A company might sell itself once. A good banking team runs sale processes constantly and knows how to create competitive tension between bidders, which directly affects price.
- **Market access.** Banks know who the likely buyers are and can reach institutional investors around the world within days. Distribution matters enormously in capital raisings.
- **Valuation and negotiation.** An independent, defensible view on value — and someone whose job is to push the other side — changes outcomes.
- **Certainty and risk management.** In an underwritten raising, the bank can guarantee the client its money even if investor demand falls short, taking that risk onto its own book for a fee.
- **Board protection.** Directors making major decisions want expert advice they can point to. On many deals a bank provides a formal opinion that the price is fair.

## Where the money comes from

Fee economics explain a lot about how banks behave and what analysts spend time on:

- **M&A advisory:** success fees typically around 0.5–2% of deal value (higher percentages on smaller deals), mostly contingent on completion.
- **Equity raisings:** roughly 1–3% of the amount raised in Australia; IPOs at the higher end because of the work and risk involved.
- **Debt raisings:** lower percentages again, but larger and more frequent transactions.

Because fees are contingent and lumpy, banks constantly **pitch** — presenting deal ideas to companies to win the next mandate. As a junior banker you will spend a meaningful share of your time on pitch materials for deals that may never happen. That is not wasted effort so much as how the business model works: pitching is the sales function of the firm.

## What this means for you

Two practical takeaways for recruiting. First, when interviewers ask "what does an investment bank do?", the strong answer is fee-focused and client-focused — advice on M&A plus raising debt and equity capital — not a vague "banks work with money". Second, understanding that the product is *transactions* explains the analyst job you are applying for: the models, company profiles and presentation pages you would build all exist to win and execute deals.
