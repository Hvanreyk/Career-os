"Finance" is a huge industry, and students often apply to investment banking without being able to say how it differs from the other careers competing for the same graduates. Interviewers test this — "why IB and not consulting?" is a genuine filter question. This lesson gives you the map.

## Advice vs balance sheet

Investment banking and commercial banking are often confused because the big Australian banks do both. The test is simple: **whose money is at risk?** A commercial bank profits from the gap between what it pays depositors and what it charges borrowers — its balance sheet is the product. An investment bank mostly risks its *time* (fee income for advice and execution), putting capital at risk only briefly, for example when underwriting a raising.

## Sell side vs buy side

Investment banking, equity research and sales & trading are the **sell side**: they create, analyse and distribute securities and transactions, selling services to investors. Private equity, asset management, hedge funds and super funds are the **buy side**: they deploy capital and make investment decisions. The distinction matters to you because the classic analyst exit path runs from sell side to buy side — IB analysts are trained in exactly the skills (modelling, valuation, deal execution) PE firms hire for.

## Deals vs positions vs advice

A second useful axis is what the day-to-day work product is:

- **IB analysts work on transactions** — long-cycle projects (weeks to months) producing models and documents, in teams, for a client outcome.
- **Traders and portfolio managers work on positions** — fast decisions with immediate feedback from the market, and P&L you can see daily.
- **Consultants work on recommendations** — structured problem-solving about strategy or operations, usually without a financial transaction at the end.

None is "better"; they suit different temperaments. If you like tangible deal outcomes and can tolerate long hours and detail-heavy work, IB fits. If you want rapid feedback and market judgement, look at S&T or funds. If you want breadth of business problems over financial depth, consulting fits better.


## The Australian wrinkle

Australia's market has a few local features worth knowing early. The "Big 4" (CBA, Westpac, NAB, ANZ) are primarily commercial banks; their institutional divisions do some debt capital markets work, but large-scale M&A advisory and ECM in Australia is led by global investment banks, Macquarie Capital and strong local independents (covered in the next lesson). Superannuation gives Australia one of the world's largest pools of managed capital, which makes buy-side clients — super funds and infrastructure investors — unusually important drivers of local deal activity.
