"Which banks should I apply to?" is the wrong first question. The right one: "which firms fit *my* situation — and what do I actually know about each?" There is no universal ranking; there is only fit, researched firm by firm.

## The research dimensions that matter

**Deal flow in your city.** What has the firm's local office actually worked on in the last 18 months? Company announcements name advisers; financial press coverage fills in the rest. A prestigious global name with a quiet Sydney desk may offer less experience than a busy independent.

**Sector strengths.** Australian offices have pronounced specialities — resources, infrastructure, financials, healthcare vary enormously by firm. If a sector draws you, weight the firms that lead it.

**Team scale and structure.** A 12-person team means analyst breadth; a 60-person office means depth and specialisation. Neither is better — know which you're choosing.

**Intern experience quality.** What do interns actually do there? Ask people who interned (Networking course): staffing model, live-deal exposure, return-offer rates, culture under pressure.

**Program mechanics.** Application window, process stages, eligibility (graduation-date rules matter for non-standard degrees), rotation structure, group allocation method.

**People and alumni.** Where do the firm's analysts go afterwards? Who from your university is there now? An alumni connection converts research into conversations.

## Sources, in order of usefulness

1. **People who work(ed) there** — the only source for culture and intern reality.
2. **Company announcements and financial press** — verifiable deal flow and adviser mandates.
3. **The firm's own careers pages** — program mechanics, dates, eligibility (primary source; check often).
4. **League tables** — directional market position; don't over-read small-market rankings.
5. **Online forums** — occasionally useful colour wrapped in noise and bravado; never load-bearing.

  Before adding any firm to your list, be able to answer: "Name one deal
