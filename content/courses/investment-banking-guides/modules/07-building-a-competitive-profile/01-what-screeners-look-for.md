Before building your profile, understand the reader. An application screener — often an analyst or HR reviewer with hundreds of resumes and minutes per candidate — is pattern-matching against a small set of signals.

## The signal hierarchy

**1. Academics.** In Australia, WAM is the first filter — a Distinction average (75+) keeps most doors open at competitive firms; High Distinction strengthens everything. ATAR still appears on penultimate-year resumes and a strong one (99+) remains a positive signal. Grades don't win the seat, but weak grades force everything else to work much harder.

**2. Relevant experience.** Anything demonstrating finance exposure and work ethic, roughly in descending signal strength: IB/PE internships (even at tiny firms), Big-4/consulting/corporate finance work, student investment funds, finance society committees, case competition results, then general employment. Note "descending signal", not "descending worth" — a long-held retail job evidences reliability better than a two-day virtual program.

**3. Leadership and initiative.** Committee roles where you *did something measurable*, starting anything (a society event, a small business, a project), sport at representative level. Titles without outcomes read as padding.

**4. Demonstrated interest.** The quiet differentiator: modelling courses completed, a personal portfolio or valuation write-ups, CFA Level 1, deals you can discuss. Screeners can't see "passion"; they can see its artefacts.

## What the screen is really asking

Behind the signals sits one question: **"Is there evidence this person can do detail-heavy analytical work, wants this industry specifically, and won't fold in month three?"** Every profile-building choice in the next lesson is just a way of manufacturing honest evidence for one of those three clauses.

```callout
variant: warning
title: What doesn't work
md: >-
  Keyword-stuffing, inflated titles, five-day virtual programs presented
  as internships, and claimed skills with no artefacts behind them.
  Screeners read hundreds of these — embellishment is obvious, checkable,
  and at interview it collapses in seconds. Build real evidence instead;
  it's not slower than faking it convincingly.
```
