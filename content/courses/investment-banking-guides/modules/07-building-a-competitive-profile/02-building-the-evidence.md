Profiles are built, not found. Here is the menu of evidence-building activities, what each one actually proves, and how to sequence them against the recruiting clock from Module 6.

## The menu, by evidence value

**Student investment funds (SMIFs).** Pitching real stocks to a committee managing real (or realistically simulated) money. Proves valuation skill, judgement and sustained commitment. Competitive to join — treat the application like a mini recruiting process.

**Case competitions.** Team-based deal or strategy problems judged by professionals, often bank-sponsored. A finals placing is a strong, dated, verifiable achievement — and the prep genuinely teaches. Even without placing, one competition per year builds skill and material for behavioural stories.

**Finance society committees.** The value is the *doing*: running events, negotiating sponsorships, managing teams. One substantive committee role beats memberships of five societies. Bonus: societies are networking infrastructure — sponsor firms send bankers to your events.

**Financial modelling.** A completed, reputable modelling course plus — more importantly — models you actually built (a DCF on a company you follow, a simple LBO). Artefacts you can discuss beat certificates you can list.

**Work experience — any work.** Retail, hospitality, tutoring: reliability, client handling, pressure. Finance-adjacent (Big 4, advisory, corporate finance, bookkeeping): directly relevant. Boutique IB exposure (Module 6's off-cycle path): transformative.

**Market awareness as a habit.** Fifteen minutes daily reading markets news, one deal followed properly per month. Free, compounding, and the difference between generic and credible interview answers.

**Certifications.** CFA Level 1 signals serious commitment (weigh the ~300 study hours against alternatives); virtual experience programs are mild positives — fine to list, never load-bearing.

## Sequencing against the clock

**Two-plus years out:** grades first (they're never cheaper to earn than now), join one or two societies properly, start the market-awareness habit, first case competition.

**One year out:** committee role or SMIF, modelling course + a personal valuation artefact, systematic networking begins, boutique off-cycle attempts, insight programs.

**Penultimate year:** applications in week one; interview prep at full intensity; keep one visible activity running (screeners notice current-year emptiness).

**Final year / graduated:** graduate applications plus the boutique track in parallel; deepen technicals; a completed personal project (portfolio write-ups, deal breakdowns) covers the experience gap better than nothing.

## Non-target and non-finance? The path exists

Australian IB hires disproportionately from a handful of universities and commerce degrees — but not exclusively, and the gap is closable with evidence:

- **Non-target university:** your grades must be excellent, and your *initiative* evidence must be undeniable — boutique internships (networking-driven, so the target-school filter is weaker), case comp results, a genuinely strong personal project. Expect to lean harder on the relationship track.
- **Non-finance degree (engineering, law, science, arts):** frame the degree as an asset — quantitative rigour, drafting skill, sector knowledge — then close the finance gap visibly: modelling course, SMIF or investment club, finance electives, CFA L1. The question you must pre-answer is "why the switch, and what have you *done* about it?"
