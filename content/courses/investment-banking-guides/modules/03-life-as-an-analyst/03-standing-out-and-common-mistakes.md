Every cohort contains the same failure modes, and interviewers sometimes probe for them pre-emptively ("tell me about a time you made a mistake under pressure"). Learn them now, cheaply.

## The classic mistakes

**Hiding problems.** The cardinal sin. You broke the model at 11pm, the deck is due at 8am — the losing move is silence and hope. Strong analysts escalate early with a proposed fix: "X is wrong, here's my plan, do you agree?"

**Guessing instead of asking (or asking instead of thinking).** Two opposite failures. Guessing at an ambiguous instruction wastes a night's work; asking questions you could answer yourself signals helplessness. The professional pattern: attempt an answer, then confirm — "I assumed X because Y; shout if that's wrong."

**Sacrificing accuracy for speed.** Sending an unchecked page five minutes earlier is never worth it. Build the habit: print-preview, tie every number to its source, sense-check magnitudes (a 4x revenue jump should trigger alarm, not a shrug).

**Poor prioritisation.** Three seniors, three "urgent" requests, one evening. Weak analysts do them in arrival order. Strong analysts triage by real deadline and impact — and *communicate*: "I have the board page for 9am and two other requests; proposing this order — okay?"

## What "standing out" actually looks like

Not heroics — compounding reliability:

- **Own your numbers.** Be the person whose pages don't need re-checking. This single trait drives analyst rankings more than any other.
- **Anticipate the next question.** If you're asked for a company's leverage, bring the peer comparison you know follows.
- **Know the deal.** Read the documents you process. Analysts who understand *why* the client cares get pulled into better work.
- **Be easy to work with at midnight.** Teams remember composure. Nobody staffs the talented analyst who becomes toxic under pressure.
- **Protect your recovery deliberately.** Sleep, exercise and real time off on quiet weekends aren't indulgences; they're what keeps quality high in month eight. Burnout produces exactly the errors that destroy trust.
