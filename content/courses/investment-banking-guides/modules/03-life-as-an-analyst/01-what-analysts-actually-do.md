Job ads say "financial analysis and transaction execution". Here is what that actually means, artefact by artefact — because interviews test whether you know what you're signing up for.

## The analyst's work products

**Company profiles.** One-to-few-page summaries of a business: what it does, financials, share price history, ownership, recent news, strategic issues. Profiles feed pitches and buyer lists. You will make many.

**Pitch books.** Slide decks presented to clients to win mandates: market updates, strategic options, valuation perspectives, credentials. Analysts build the pages — charts, tables, "spreads" of comparable companies — and process endless rounds of senior comments. A meaningful share of pitch work never becomes a live deal; that's the business model, not wasted effort.

**Financial models.** Excel models that forecast a company's financials and value it: three-statement operating models, DCFs, merger (accretion/dilution) models, LBO models. Building them accurately and *auditable* — so a VP can trace every number — is the core hard skill of the job.

**Process materials.** On live deals: information memoranda, management presentations, buyer/investor lists, due-diligence trackers, data-room management, working-group lists, board papers. Less glamorous, absolutely central — deals run on this machinery, and analysts run the machinery.

**Analysis on demand.** "What has this company said about margins?" "How did the market react to competitor X's downgrade?" "Spread the last five deals in the sector." Fast, accurate answers to questions like these build a reputation quickly.

## The hierarchy and where you sit

A typical deal team: **Analyst** (you) → **Associate** (checks and directs your work, often 2–4 years in) → **Vice President** (runs day-to-day execution and drafting) → **Director / Executive Director** → **Managing Director** (owns the client relationship, wins the business). Your work flows *up* this chain, and comments flow *down* it. Most of your direct interaction is with associates and VPs; MD exposure grows as you prove reliable.

## The honest lifestyle picture

The hours are real: weekday evenings are routinely consumed, and live deals take weekends. Australian hours are generally somewhat better than New York's but materially worse than almost any other graduate job. Workload arrives unevenly — a quiet Tuesday can become a 1am Thursday because a client meeting moved. Two things make it sustainable for those who last: genuinely liking the content of the work, and teams that respect protected time where they can.
