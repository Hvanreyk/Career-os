## Start with what you did

A useful bullet normally lets the reader identify your action, its context and why it mattered. You do not need to force every bullet into one formula, but the subject should be your contribution rather than a vague description of the organisation.

## Add evidence you can defend

Evidence may be a result, scale, frequency, audience, constraint or method. Numbers help only when they are accurate and meaningful. Never estimate a metric simply because a rewrite looks stronger with one.

```profile_example
strength: weak
title: Generic responsibility
bullets:
  - Responsible for helping with financial analysis and presentations.
verdict: The reader cannot see the specific analysis, audience or contribution.
```

```profile_example
strength: strong
title: Fictional evidence-led example
bullets:
  - Analysed three acquisition scenarios for a student case competition and presented the valuation trade-offs to a four-person judging panel.
verdict: The example is specific because its fictional context, action and audience are clear; a real student must use only their own facts.
```

## Revise, then defend it aloud

After editing, explain the bullet without reading it. If the wording implies work you did not perform, remove or correct it. Interview defensibility is a useful truth test.
